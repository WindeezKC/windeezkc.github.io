HydroDrip

Taegun Harshbarger, Swasti Mishra, Marco Deleon, and Kellan Christ

Abstract

Few things sound as compelling as never killing a beloved houseplant again. With the help of this device, that dream can be a reality.

Using the HydroDrip is as simple as just turning the device on, placing the tube and moisture sensor in the soil of the plant, and then filling a water bottle and placing it in the bottle port. This device will keep the plant alive by automatically watering it when the soil is too dry.

Furthermore, because there are no wires to charge the device, it becomes a lot more convenient for the average person to use. The solar panel charges the battery, and users only have to refill the bottle. This report will cover a few different topics related to the development of this device, including an explanation of the original problem and the team’s objectives, market research, the methods and tools used, a description of the team’s results, and a discussion of the next steps. Further, this report will conclude with references and appendices containing more information about the HydroDrip.

Introduction

Plants play an essential role in humans' everyday lives, providing oxygen, food, and aesthetic value. However, maintaining healthy plants can be challenging, especially for individuals with busy schedules or those who lack the necessary knowledge or expertise to manage them. Automatic plant watering systems have become increasingly popular in recent years, somewhat addressing this need. However, automatic plant watering solutions have plenty of limitations– some are analog, and don’t conform to a plant’s actual requirements, some are simply based on time rather than the moisture of the soil, and some use reservoirs, which deplete very quickly.

This report describes the development of an automatic plant waterer system that utilizes an ESP32 microcontroller, a tube, a solar panel, and a battery to water plants efficiently and sustainably. The project's main objective is to provide a low-cost user and eco-friendly solution to the challenges associated with maintaining healthy plants. The system is designed to read the soil's moisture level and activate the water pump accordingly, eliminating the guesswork involved in plant care. The use of solar power also makes the system environmentally friendly and sustainable, and using a water bottle instead of a reservoir also means that the device uses less water than other solutions currently on the market. This project's importance lies in its potential to democratize plant care, making it more accessible to individuals with various levels of expertise and busy schedules. By providing an easy-to-use and efficient solution to plant care, this project aims to promote a greener and healthier future.

Market Research & Existing Work

Many of our competitors’ products fail in one critical aspect, which is that they do not test the soil moisture levels when watering the plant. A few competitors also just test moisture levels without watering the plant. All the products on the market that automatically water plants use a pump and timer to achieve their goal, or if they do not, they are simply just gravity-powered and either drip water at regular intervals or ‘leak’ into the plant. Further, with many of the regular drip designs, moisture isn’t accounted for if, for instance, it has recently rained.

Also, for many competing designs, there is usually a bowl the user has to place the device or a tube in, and not a designated device to hold the water. Our design, on the other hand, has an all-in-one functionality, allowing for a user to refill and swap the water bottles by screwing them in as well as testing the moisture level so that it only waters the plant when it is too dry. The team is also using solar power to charge the battery in the HydroDrip so that the battery remains charged and does not have to be swapped out. ​​Due to these differences, HydroDrip is truly a different product from its competitors.

        

In these two products and products like them, the design utilizes a glass or bowl and has timers to water the plant at a certain frequency set by the user. This does not allow for a system to know if the plant requires more water because it is timed rather than dynamically sensing. For example, if it were to rain or if the soil was to get unnaturally wet or dry, these designs would not be able to respond adequately. The first product is the Tecnovo Automatic Watering System for Potted Plants [1], and the second product is the Moistenland Automatic Irrigation System [2]. Both of these products and similar ones are easily found on Amazon.

This design uses some Arduino products and again uses a glass of water to feed water into the soil. While this device does have a moisture sensor, it also uses a glass of water making it less of an all-in-one system than the product the team designed. This self-made project is called the Arduino Plant Watering System [3].

 

Here are two designs that are gravity fed, again with no use of power and no way to test the moisture levels which can lead to either under-watering or over-watering. This second design, however, does use a water bottle to swap the water tank. Based on sales of this design, it does not seem to be popular as most people go for automatic systems instead. This product is a Lorvain Automatic Plant Watering Globes [4] and the second is the Self Plant Watering Spikes 12 Pack Auto Drippers Irrigation Devices Vacation Automatic Plants Water System with Adjustable Control Valve Switch Design for Houseplant [5].

Based on an analysis of our product versus existing designs, our design incorporates all the functionality a user would ever need to support their plants. It can test the soil for moisture levels, be aware of when to water the plant, and eliminate the guessing game involved with watering a plant. Our device will provide the correct amount of water no matter what time of day it is, and when a user notices that the water reservoir is too low, they can simply unscrew the bottle and replace it with a new one. With the solar panel’s ability to charge the battery, our design can keep a longer charge than the products that run solely on battery. This is because, during the day, our product charges the battery, which requires less maintenance from a user. For these reasons, building a singular plant watering system that is completely automatic and communicates via Bluetooth to a user’s computer or phone is an innovative idea.

With the addition of moisture sensors, a solar panel to charge the battery,  an automatic watering system, and communication to a user's phone or laptop, the user can set what plant they are watering, change the moisture levels, and see what the current moisture levels are. The user only has to worry about the water bottle and water level; they never have to worry about battery life or making sure their plant is correctly watered.

Methodology

It took many steps to get the Hydro Drip to a polished product prototype.

  • First, the team had to figure out what the best water and moisture levels were for different plants. The team wanted users to be able to switch between different plants and have the HydroDrip water the plant based on its preferred moisture level.
  • Next, the team had to design and plan. The team figured out what parts were needed: a solar panel, a DC pump, a 5v relay, a moisture sensor, and so on.
  • Prototyping the product allowed the team to see how much space was needed to fit everything into one box while also capturing the look the team wanted for the product.
  • The team performed data analysis on the device’s water output and the moisture level plants needed. It took several trials to figure out how soon after dispensing water the device would need to check for moisture levels again. Eventually, this was figured out and the moisture threshold for each plant was determined.
  • At this point, the team deployed the HydroDrip and began monitoring the device to make sure it was performing correctly. And it was!

Many design changes were made throughout the process. The original solar panel was too weak to charge the batteries, so it was replaced with a more powerful panel. Further, the tubing was originally too long, so the team cut it shorter. Lastly, holes were added at the end of the tube to prevent the backpressure from building up.

The parts used for the product are as follows:

  • An Arduino IDE was used to program the ESP32 board.
  • A 5v relay was used to control the pump and solar panel.
  • A solar energy manager and battery were used to charge the battery of the solar panel as well as supply power to all the components including the 5v relay, DC pump, and ESP32.
  • A 3D printer was used for prototyping and printing the final housing for the device.
  • Three LEDs were used to show what the moisture levels were in the plant and the Bluetooth connection status of the device.
  • Silicon caulk, water bottles, nuts, and bolts were also used to protect the moisture sensor while the silicon was used to create a waterproof seal.

Much of the circuit work was done before designing any of the housing. Going into it, the team knew that the housing would have to be at least as big as the solar panel. Multiple sketches were developed to get an idea of how the product would look and how the team would feed water into the pump. After wiring, testing, and drawing, the team made some simple CAD designs and printed them out. After this was done the team set everything up inside the housing before adhering it in place to make sure it fit. The only issue with the housing was the type-c USB port of the solar manager was misaligned, but this was easily fixed. With the solar panel flush on the side and the pump mounted, everything worked fine and there were no signs of leakage. After this initial testing, the team was happy with the design and implementation of the product.

Results

The automatic plant waterer system utilizing an ESP32 microcontroller, a tube, a solar panel, and a battery has been successfully developed and tested. The system was designed to read the moisture level of the soil and activate the water pump accordingly, use a solar panel to charge the battery and be eco-friendly and sustainable. The following results were obtained:

  1. Moisture Sensing: The system's moisture sensing capabilities were tested, and it was found that the ESP32 was able to read the moisture level of the soil. The system was programmed to activate the water pump when the moisture level fell below a specific threshold.
  2. Watering Accuracy: The accuracy of the system in watering the plants was also tested. The system was able to dispense the correct amount of water needed to keep the plants moisturized without overwatering or underwatering them.
  3. Power Efficiency: The use of a solar panel to power the system was found to be highly efficient, with the system running on a battery charged by solar power for an extended period without the need for external power.

Challenges encountered during the project included issues with the water pump's suction ability when the flowerpot was too high (siphoning occurred) and initial difficulties with programming the ESP32. These issues were overcome by adjusting the system's design and revising the code. Notable achievements and innovations included the use of a solar panel to power the system, making the water bottles swappable, and overall having the system be eco-friendly, sustainable, and user-friendliness.

In conclusion, the HydroDrip was found to be an effective and efficient solution to plant care. It has the ability to automate the watering process, read moisture levels, and operate on solar power. The project's success demonstrates the potential of utilizing technology to promote sustainable and eco-friendly solutions to everyday problems.

Discussion

The results of this project demonstrate the successful development of an automatic plant waterer system. The device’s ability to read soil moisture levels and activate the water pump accordingly, coupled with its use of solar power, makes it a sustainable solution to plant care.

One significant limitation of this project is the system's inability to detect and address other factors that may impact plant health, such as temperature and lighting. Additionally, the system's size and design may limit its application to gardens or indoor environments with many plants. Future directions for research and development include incorporating sensors to measure temperature and light levels and scaling up the system to accommodate gardens or commercial settings.

The impact of this project on its intended audience or users is significant. The system's user-friendliness makes it accessible to individuals with varying levels of expertise and busy schedules. Further, the use of hot-swappable water bottle technology also aligns with the growing demand for environmentally conscious solutions. The system's ability to automate the watering process also frees up time for users, promoting a more delightful plant-owning experience.

In conclusion, the HydroDrip presents a successful and innovative solution to plant care. While the system has limitations, its potential to be developed further and improve the quality of life for plant owners is significant. The project's impact on its intended audience or users is promising, as it offers a sustainable and user-friendly solution to plant care, promoting a greener and healthier future.

Conclusion

The HydroDrip is a unique and necessary solution to the problem of keeping plants alive without having the manually water them. Additionally, the use of the solar panel also encourages users to ensure that the plant is in a bright enough atmosphere for it to thrive. This report has included information for users with busy schedules or those without plant expertise to manage their indoor environments and experience the joy of plant ownership.

    pinMode(LowMoistureLED, OUTPUT);

 pinMode(btLED, OUTPUT);

}

 }
